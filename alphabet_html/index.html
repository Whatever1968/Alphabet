About us

Accolades

Founder Monica Golendrina, featured in Raising California Together Magazine,

A SENSE OF ACCOMPLISHMENT: When I was a Child, I had a learning disability. back then people didn't really put names on things like that, so it was just really hard. I see a lot of children who have learning challenges like I did. Some of my afterschool kids try so hard to get out of doing homework-it's exasperating to them. I like to make learning fun so that they're motivated to go after learning. One of my students would tell me that he can't read, but his parents said otherwise. He just hated reading so much. So I encouraged this kid to read what he wanted, which was books with fewer words. And then he discovered a pile of flashcards--just a few little individual pieces of paper with words. He felt more of a sense of accomplishment going through the whole pile of words. Now he is reading a lot better.

INDIVIDUAL ATTENTION: I teach interpersonal communication so children don't feel like they always have to go to a teacher to get help. Nobody tattles here after they've been with me a while--they just help each other. If I was teaching a class of 20 or more kids, I wouldn't be able to give that individual attention. I keep my classes small.

Reading Time

Did You Know?

Did you know that 68% of America’s fourth graders do not read at a proficient level? How about the fact that one out of six children who do not read at age level by the end of third grade will not graduate from high school?

Age Group

Half of all low-income fourth-graders score below basic levels on U.S. literacy assessments. And yet, more than 60% of low-income families can't afford to have books in their homes.

What were doing.

We know that fostering a love of learning early on is key to ensuring our children's success in school and life.

Play Time

Did you know?

Play is essential to development because it contributes to the cognitive, physical, social, and emotional well-being of children and youth. Play also offers an ideal opportunity for parents to engage fully with their children.

Age Group

Although everyone benefits, play therapy is especially appropriate for children ages 3 through 12 years old (Carmichael, 2006; Gil, 1991; Landreth, 2002; Schaefer, 1993).

What were doing.

Each play therapy session varies in length but usually last about 30 to 50 minutes. Sessions are usually held daily.

Music

Did you know?

Singing and music play an important role in our culture. You'll find music present in many aspects of our lives. But none is as important as how we use music with our children.

Age Group

Most school-age children are intrigued by songs that involve counting, spelling, or remembering a sequence of events. Songs and musical activities with other school subjects also are effective during this child developmental stage. School-age children begin expressing their likes and dislikes of different types of music. They may express an interest in taking musical lessons.

What were doing.

There is no downside to bringing children and music together. From the pure pleasure of listening to soothing sounds and rhythmic harmonies, to gaining new language and social skills, whatever the setting - a quiet room or large grassy field or a busy classroom - music can enliven and enrich the lives of children and the people who care for them.

Exercise

Did you know?

Parents surely know that more outdoor play time will help to balance out a child’s day and offer them mental and physical health benefits but how much is enough?

Age Group

A recent study by the Kaiser Family Foundation found that the average eight to eighteen year old kid is now spending an alarming 7 hours and 38 minutes of electronic screen time per day.

What were doing.

The Centers for Disease Control advises parents to encourage their children to have an hour per day of moderate physical activity to burn calories, tone the muscles and keep their weight under control. Outdoor play time is ideal for this.

Learning

Did you know?

As educators know children's growth and development follow certain patterns. Just as children's height, weight, and physical abilities change, so do their attention span, their sense of humor and their tendency to prefer large or small groups in social settings.

Age Group

In The Benefits of Mixed Age Grouping (1995), Katz provides a clear understanding of what "mixed-age grouping" means. She writes that "Although humans are not usually born in litters, we seem to insist that they be educated in them." In most families children are born one at a time, and if the parents opt to have more than one child, the children are spaced out over a few years. In the home, the older children help the younger ones with certain tasks. In this helping relationship, the younger and older children work together to help the younger learn new skills.

What were doing.

When we design classrooms, lessons, curricular projects, and routines according to our knowledge of students' strengths and needs, we create classrooms that allow students to be more succssful, both socially and academically.
